How We Work

A PROCESS
BUILT ON TRUST.

Whether it’s a new backyard court or resurfacing an existing facility, we follow the same proven process on every project. No surprises. No excuses.

01

Site Evaluation & Planning

We visit your site, assess the existing surface or plan the new one, and design the layout. Court orientation, drainage, net system type, and color scheme are all decided before work begins. No surprises mid-project.

02

Surface Prep

Cracks filled. Low spots leveled. Existing coatings evaluated. If the concrete needs repair or the base isn’t right, we address it before a single coat goes down. This is where most resurface jobs fail. We don’t skip it.

03

Surfacing & Striping

4–8 coats of premium pre-pigmented acrylic, each fully cured before the next. Proper texture for your desired ball bounce. Crisp lines. Custom colors if you want them. No watered-down franchise paint.

04

Nets, Accessories & Walkthrough

Net system installed (permanent or portable. We’ll recommend the right one for your use case). Windscreens, accessories, and finishing touches. Final walkthrough with you before we leave.

Net Systems Explained

PORTABLE VS. PERMANENT
PICKLEBALL NETS.

This is one of the most common questions we get during , and the answer depends on how you’ll actually use the court. Here’s our honest take.

Best For Most Homes

Portable / Semi-Permanent

  • Switch from pickleball to basketball to scooters in under a minute
  • No sleeves poured into the concrete. One fewer place for cracks to start
  • Simplifies the build and saves money on installation
  • Durable semi-permanent systems play as well as permanent ones
  • Keeps your options open as your family’s needs change
  • Budget: ~$2,500 for a durable rolling system
Best For Dedicated Facilities

Permanent Posts

  • Immovable. Poured into concrete sleeves, won’t shift or blow in wind
  • Right choice when the court is purpose-built for pickleball only
  • Standard for country clubs, universities, and public parks
  • 10+ year lifespan with zero maintenance
  • Cleaner look on a single-sport facility
  • Budget: ~$2,000+ including labor and materials

We install both systems and we’ll tell you honestly which one fits your project. The best time to choose is during the design phase, not after the concrete is poured.

Frequently Asked

PICKLEBALL COURT QUESTIONS?
START HERE.

The questions we hear most from St. George homeowners and facility managers planning pickleball court surfacing or construction.

How much does it cost to surface a pickleball court? +

Surfacing an existing court typically runs $8,000–$15,000 depending on court condition, surface prep needed, and the coating system. A full new build (including concrete, surfacing, fencing, and accessories) runs $25,000–$60,000+ depending on site conditions.

We give honest, line-item estimates after a site visit. No mystery pricing. And we’ll tell you if a resurface isn’t the right move. Sometimes a full rebuild makes more sense for your budget and timeline.

Should we choose a permanent or portable net system? +

For most residential courts, portable or semi-permanent nets are the better choice. They let you use the court for other activities, eliminate concrete sleeves (one fewer crack point), and cost about the same as permanent posts.

Permanent posts are the right call for dedicated facilities: country clubs, public parks, and courts that will only ever be used for pickleball. We install both and we’ll recommend the right one for your situation.

Can we convert our tennis court to pickleball? +

Almost always yes, and it’s the fastest, most affordable way to get pickleball courts. A standard tennis court can accommodate up to four pickleball courts depending on size, fence layout, and surface condition.

But conversion isn’t as simple as painting new lines. Court dimensions, net post placement, player run-off zones, and concrete condition all need to be evaluated. We’ll map the optimal layout and tell you honestly what fits, and what doesn’t.

How do I choose a court contractor? +

Five years ago there were four real court contractors in Utah. Today there are dozens of , and very little real experience behind most of them. Here are the questions that separate the pros from the pretenders:

  • How many years have you actually been surfacing courts in Utah? Not how long the website , but how many seasons of crews on courts.
  • What materials do you use, and are they pre-pigmented or mixed on site? Pre-pigmented systems hold color far longer.
  • Will you manage the full project or just show up to paint? A court is only as good as the prep underneath the coating.
  • What’s your warranty, and will you still be in business to honor it?

One thing worth knowing: Utah has a local franchise that sells low-cost court paint to anyone who with no qualifications, no training. That’s the single biggest reason there are so many “court contractors” now, and it’s the single biggest reason we get called to tear out failed courts and start over. Ask what brand of materials your contractor uses, and ask why.
